Biofuel

Biofuel is a renewable energy source produced from plant and animal matter called biomass

Biofuels can exist in solid, liquid, or gas phases. This makes them extremely practical for substituting for fossil fuels like coal, oil, and natural gas (Lehmin and Selin).The most common types of biofuel are as listed:

1. Ethanol
Ethanol is a type of alcohol and is used mostly in the liquid state.
It is produced from plant material such as corn and sugarcane and is created from the fermentation process of bacteria and yeast which turn plant sugars into the ethanol.
Ethanol is particularly useful because it can be mixed with gasoline to act as a fuel for motor vehicles. If you've ever seen a sign at a gas station read E10 or E15 or something similar this is referring to the concentration of ethanol (E10 is 10% ethanol and 90% gasoline). You've also probably noticed that the larger the E number the cheaper the fuel, this is because ethanol is cheaper than gasoline and so more ethanol means cheaper fuel (Energy.gov).

2. Biodiesel
Biodiesel is another liquid biofuel and is similar to ethanol in its uses.
It is made from soybean oil and animal fats. Biodiesel can also be mixed with diesel and it is labeled as B Number like B50 or B75. The nice thing about biodiesel is that it can be used a complete replacement to diesel and B100 can be used by most cars. This is unlike ethanol which can't reach high concentrations for most cars (Energy.gov).

3. Wood
Wood is a solid biofuel and has already been used by humans for thousands of years to create energy. Wood produces heat when it is burned which can be used by power generators to produce electricity. Note that wood is not limited to trees but some other plants as well (Techie Scientist).

4. Biogas
Biogas is a gaseous biofuel that is mostly made up of methane and carbon dioxide. It is produced by bacteria as a byproduct which can use leftover waste to create the biogas, which is why it is often created near landfills. Compared to alternative gaseous fuels like methane, biogas has a sharp reduction in greenhouse gas emissions (Badurek).

Benefits

Biofuels largest positive is its versatile applications, from powering cars to homes.
Biofuels can reduce gas prices by serving as an alternative, decrease emissions, and allow us to reuse waste.

Biofuel is also readily available and can be produced on commercial farms (Lehmin and Selin).


Limitations

The most controversial aspect of biofuels is that production is resource-intensive. Biofuel is not mined but rather grown or cultivated, meaning the production of biofuel takes away from the food supply, allowing for a significant amount of food to be wasted on fuel rather than consumption. While biofuel is green itself, the equipment used to produce it emits a considerable amount of fossil fuels (Lehmin and Selin).

Relevant Chemical Equations

  • Fermentation of Sucrose to Create Ethanol
    C₆H₁₀O₆ (aq)→ 2C₂H₅OH (aq) + 2CO₂ (g)
    Here, sucrose is being broken down into ethanol, releasing carbon dioxide as a by product (Energy.gov).
  • Combustion of Wood
    CⱼHₖOₗ (s) + O₂ (g) → H₂O (g) + CO₂ (g) + ΔH
    Since wood is made up of many types of carbohydrates, we denote the subscripts with variable amounts. Here wood is reacting with oxygen as well as an initial burst of heat (spark) to create water vapor, carbon dioxide, and heat (Techie Scientist).
