Nuclear Power

Nuclear Power harnesses attracting forces inside every atom, releasing large quantities of energy.

Nuclear power is harnessed by dividing an atom, the smallest unit of matter. Fission reactors, the main type of reactor utilized today, divides Uranium-235 isotopes into Krypton-92 and Barium-139, while releasing three neutrons to continue the reaction.

1. Initiation
Rods made out of enriched Uranium, with a higher concentration of U-235, are entered into the reactor and wait for the initiation of the reaction. Control rods are lowered, so the reaction doesn't start before the plant is ready, and the startup neutron source is introduced (Duke Energy).
This source can be Californium-252 or Plutonium-238. Once the control rods are raised, the reaction can begin.

2. The Chain Reaction
Once the reaction begins, it keeps going. Each reaction prompts the next one in the chain, as three additional neutrons are emitted, which can cause three more additional reactions. Control rods, often made out of Boron or Cadmium, absorb excess neutrons, so that the critical mass doesn't become supercritical and get out of control(US Dept. of Energy).
Since reactors only change 1/3 of their fuel supply at a time (Duke Energy), there isn't often a reason to turn off the reactor. This means that the reaction keeps going and there is no reason to turn it off. This is where the heat is produced.

3. Using the Energy and Storage of Materials
After the heat is created, it uses a conventional means to create electricity. This heat makes water boil, and the steam spins a turbine to produce usable energy that goes into the power grid. 
Spent fuel rods are stored inside a pool of water known as a spent fuel pool. The water engages as a neutron-absorbing material, a coolant, and a moderator (US Nuclear Regulation Committee). This prevents the reaction from continuing, and cools the hot uranium. In fact, a nuclear power plant is so safe, it is possible to both swim and drink the water used in a nuclear reactor (Texas A&M)!

4. Shutting the Reactor Down
Should the reactor need to shut down, the control rods are lowered such that the Uranium becomes subcritical, and the reaction dies out as all neutrons are absorbed(German Federal Office for the Safety of Nuclear Waste Management).

Benefits

Nuclear energy is one of the most reliable forms of energy (US Dept. of Energy). It does not require any external weather condition to operate, and has a very reliable energy output. 
Once a nuclear power plant is built, they are easy and affordable to maintain compared to other energy sources (IAEA 109).


Limitations

Nuclear power plants have a stigma of being very risky to operate. The horror stories of Chernobyl, Three Mile Island, and Fukushima, have discouraged nations from investing in the energy source.
Nuclear power plants are expensive to build upfront (Schlissel and Biewald), and do not have a permanent waste disposal method (Princeton).

Relevant Chemical Equation

  • The main chemical equation is the fission of Uranium-235. This occurs when a neutron hits a Uranium-235 atom, and briefly creates Uranium-236.
  • This is a very unstable compound, and 85% of the time, it breaks down into Barium-139 and Krypton-94, while releasing 3 neutrons and heat.
  • The other 15% of the time, Uranium-236 is formed, and a gamma ray, a very dangerous form of radiation, is released. This radiation is properly contained by reactor shielding, coolant, and the reactor building itself.

Wallach, Nuclear Reactors Around the World

Usage

The Nuclear Energy Institute writes “Nuclear produces more of America’s carbon-free electricity than all other clean energy sources combined.” Nuclear energy can and is implemented almost everywhere because of its lack of geographic or meteorological requirements. From eastern states like New Jersey and Illinois to California, nuclear energy is implemented throughout the nation (EIA). Internationally, nuclear energy accounts for over 70% of France’s energy (World Nuclear) and has notably been used in the UK, China, and Japan (Wallach).
